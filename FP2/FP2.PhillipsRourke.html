Layouts

Pie chart

from

https://bl.ocks.org/mbostock/3887235

We can use the pie chart to answer questions about distribution. If each slice is an issue area, it could show which issue areas the court hears cases from. It could also show individual legal provisions, but I think that could result in too many different slices for the pie chart. In terms of interactivity, rather than looking at the court's history as a whole, a slider could change the chief justice/era and look at how this has changed over time. Using a donut chart rather than a pie chart could be visually helpful in our case, as we could display information in the center such as the total number of cases, or a portrait of the chief justice




Scatterplot

from https://bost.ocks.org/mike/nations/

A scatterplot a scatterplot could answer a number of questions, but specifically, it could examine how liberal or conservative the court leans throughout the years, by plotting the number of liberal decisions in each year in one color, and the number of conservative decisions in another color, versus time. Alternatively, rather than two different dots, there could be one dot that reflects an average of the conservative and liberal decisions in a certain year on a scale of -1 to 1, so years that are more liberal might be closer to 1 and years that are more conservative might be closer to -1.
Alternatively, the scatterplot could show how individual justices have changed their voting preferences over time (though this might be better represented by a line graph, explained later), and could be refined to look at only certain issue areas whether for an individual justice or the court as a whole.


Bar Chart

from https://bl.ocks.org/mbostock/3885304

A bar chart could again answer many questions, but specifically, it could examine which justices promote unity. The height of the would be the average number of votes in the majority opinion, and the x axis would be each chief justice. Again, this could be refined by issue area.
Alternatively, the bar chart could tackle the question of overruling precedent, mapping in the frequency of precedent being overruled in given certain years or chief justices. For this visualization to reveal interesting trends, clicking on the bar could reveal information about which legal provisions were actually overturned, or the issue areas they fall under.

Stacked Bar Chart

from https://bl.ocks.org/mbostock/3886208
also http://www.bowdoin.edu/~congdon/Courses/3665/Share/CancerStack/stackedBar.html

Rather than relying on interactivity in the bar chart to show different issue areas, the stacked bar chart could be split by issue area. Alternatively, it could be split by conservative/liberal decision, to show whether a chief justice was more concerned with unity in a certain direction than the other.


Line Graph

from https://bl.ocks.org/mbostock/3884955
also https://bl.ocks.org/mbostock/3883245
also http://www.bowdoin.edu/~congdon/Courses/3665/Share/Stocks/MultiLineHaDang/MultiLine.html
and http://www.bowdoin.edu/~congdon/Courses/3665/Share/Wines/map_projection_distortions/part2.html

As mentioned earlier, a line graph could be used to show the voting preferences of individual justices over time. Because the points are connected, it could be more visually appealing in displaying this relationship. Additionally, it could plot multiple justice's at once, with a different color, so they can be compared individually - perhaps every justice under a certain chief justice, in that ~10-30 year term.


Heat Map

from https://bl.ocks.org/tlfrd/cfb80089b4d55b21f1ce18bae6842fff
also http://www.bowdoin.edu/~congdon/Courses/3665/Share/ElNino/Heatmap/proj2-grid.html

A heat map could be another way to show the voting preferences of each chief justice. With every justice on the Y axis, and every year on the x axis, the color of each box could represent how liberal or conservative they voted in that year. Again, this could be refined by issue era, or by chief justice.


Choropleth

from https://bl.ocks.org/mbostock/4060606
also http://www.bowdoin.edu/~congdon/Courses/3665/Share/Fires/P2Phillips/p2p1.html

The choropleth could be used to examine any number of questions relating to geography. Specifically, it could answer where cases are most likely to originate from. At a county or state level, a color scale could reflect the frequency of cases originating in a certain area.


Tree Map

from https://bl.ocks.org/mbostock/4063582

Because there are so many individual legal provisions, a tree map could be used to look at information in regards to these where a bar chart would be too busy. It can show which legal provisions are most contentious overall, or in certain years.
